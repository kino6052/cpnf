# Appendix C: Clarification on Sieve-Theoretic Error Terms and the Validity of the CPNF Argument

_This appendix addresses a common misconception regarding the application of sieve theory in the Computational Prime Number Framework (CPNF), particularly concerning the behavior of error terms in sieve methods when the modulus is fixed._

## C.1: Acknowledgment of the Structural Argument Gap

The elementary structural argument presented in Section 4.4 (Theorem 2) of the main paper relies on lattice uniformity properties to rule out the "endless chase" scenario. While this argument provides valuable intuition about why the twin‑prime certification process should continue indefinitely, it contains a logical gap that was correctly identified by our critic.

**The Gap**: The argument assumes that once the certification window \(W(y)\) exceeds the fixed modulus \(M_0\), it must contain an admissible twin index. However, admissibility in CPNF is stage‑relative and non‑monotonic: an index that is admissible at one stage may become inadmissible at a later stage when new blockers are added. Therefore, demonstrating the existence of admissible indices in the window does not, by itself, guarantee that any particular index will remain admissible long enough to be certified.

We acknowledge this limitation in the elementary argument. The structural reasoning alone is insufficient to prove the non‑termination of the twin‑sifting process.

## C.2: The Sieve‑Theoretic Argument Remains Valid

The core proof of the paper's main result does not depend on the elementary structural argument. Instead, it relies on the sieve‑theoretic proof presented in **Appendix B**, which uses the Fundamental Lemma of Sieve Theory (Halberstam–Richert, 1974). This argument is mathematically sound and addresses the concerns raised about error terms.

### C.2.1: The Fundamental Lemma's Guarantee

For a sieve of dimension \(\kappa = 2\) (each prime \(p\) removes exactly two residue classes), the Fundamental Lemma provides unconditional constants \(c_1, c_2 > 0\) such that for all sufficiently large \(y\):

\[
\#\{ n \le W(y) : \forall p \le y,\ 6n \not\equiv \pm 1 \pmod{p} \}
\ge c_1 \frac{W(y)}{(\log y)^2}.
\tag{C.1}
\]

This lower bound holds **deterministically** for any choice of two forbidden residue classes per prime, without requiring any distributional assumptions or unproven conjectures.

### C.2.2: Error Term Analysis

A common misunderstanding is that the error term in sieve methods grows exponentially with the number of sifting primes, making the lower bound ineffective for our parameters. This misconception arises from considering the full inclusion‑exclusion formula without truncation.

The Fundamental Lemma avoids this exponential explosion by using a **truncated inclusion‑exclusion** with a carefully chosen truncation level \(D\). Specifically:

1. After fixing the modulus \(M_0\), we sift the arithmetic progression:
   \[
   A = \{ n \le W(y) : n \equiv r \pmod{M_0} \text{ for some } r \in R_0 \}
   \]
   where \(R_0\) is the set of admissible residues modulo \(M_0\) at the freezing stage.

2. The size of \(A\) is approximately \(X = W(y)/M_0 \sim y^2/M_0\).

3. The Fundamental Lemma chooses a truncation parameter \(s > 0\) and sets \(D = X^{1/s}\). For our parameters, taking \(s = 2\) yields \(D \sim y/\sqrt{M_0}\).

4. The remainder terms in the sieve are then bounded by the number of divisors \(d\) of the sifting primes with \(d < D\), which is at most \(D\). Consequently, the total error term is \(O(D) = O(y)\).

5. The main term in (C.1) is of order \(y^2/(\log y)^2\), which dominates the \(O(y)\) error term for all sufficiently large \(y\).

Thus, contrary to the critic's claim, the error term does **not** grow exponentially with \(\pi(y)\); it grows polynomially (linearly) in \(y\). This is a standard result in modern sieve theory and is precisely why the Fundamental Lemma provides effective lower bounds.

## C.3: Why the Parity Barrier is Irrelevant

The parity problem in sieve theory concerns the inability of linear sieve methods to distinguish between numbers with an even number of prime factors and those with an odd number. This creates a barrier when trying to **count primes exactly** (numbers with exactly one prime factor).

In CPNF, we are not trying to count primes. We are counting numbers that avoid two specific residue classes modulo each prime—a sieve of dimension 2. The Fundamental Lemma provides unconditional lower bounds for such sieves, regardless of parity considerations. The parity problem is therefore irrelevant to the CPNF argument.

## C.4: Mathematical Verification

Let us verify the growth rates explicitly:

- Main term: \(\displaystyle \frac{W(y)}{(\log y)^2} \sim \frac{y^2}{6(\log y)^2}\)
- Error term: \(O(y)\)

For large \(y\), we have:
\[
\frac{y^2}{6(\log y)^2} \gg y
\]
Indeed, dividing both sides by \(y\) gives:
\[
\frac{y}{6(\log y)^2} \gg 1
\]
which holds for all \(y > e^{\sqrt{6}} \approx 403\). Thus, for \(y > 403\), the main term dominates the error term, and the lower bound (C.1) is positive and growing.

## C.5: Implications for the CPNF Process

Since the set of indices admissible for the current blocker set \(B_k\) is a **superset** of the set counted in (C.1) (because \(B_k \subseteq \{p \le y_k\}\)), the lower bound applies to CPNF-admissible indices as well. As \(y_k \to \infty\), the number of admissible indices in \([1, W(y_k)]\) grows without bound.

The CPNF process certifies twin indices in increasing order. Since there are only finitely many already-certified indices, the growing lower bound guarantees that eventually an admissible index not yet certified must appear in the window. By the deterministic rules of CPNF, this index will be certified within finitely many steps.

## C.6: Conclusion

The critic's objection correctly identifies a gap in the elementary structural argument of Section 4.4, and we acknowledge that this part of the paper should be revised or noted as heuristic. However, the paper's main result does not depend on that argument. The sieve‑theoretic proof in Appendix B, based on the Fundamental Lemma of Sieve Theory, provides a rigorous and unconditional lower bound that forces the CPNF twin‑sifting process to continue indefinitely.

The misunderstanding about error terms stems from a confusion between the full inclusion‑exclusion formula (which has exponential complexity) and the truncated version used in modern sieve theory (which yields polynomial errors). In the CPNF framework, with parameters \(W(y) \sim y^2\) and sifting by primes up to \(y\), the Fundamental Lemma guarantees a positive and growing lower bound for all sufficiently large \(y\), establishing the non‑termination of the twin‑prime certification process.

We thank the critic for engaging with our work and for providing the opportunity to clarify these important mathematical points.
